# Cold Call Framework

4-step structure for outbound cold calls. Every call follows: Opener > Question > Close > Discovery.

## Step 1: Opener (Permission-Based)

Concise, clear, easy to understand. Keep tone level (no up tones). Two proven formats:

**Format A: Acknowledge + Promise**
```
Hey {name}, it's Emma with CoPilot AI.
I know you didn't expect my call.
Do you have a moment? I promise to be brief.
```

**Format B: Upfront + Context**
```
Hey {name}, it's Emma with CoPilot AI.
I'll be upfront with you. This is a cold call about [context: e.g. LinkedIn prospecting for your sales team].
Do you have 30 seconds to hear why I'm reaching out to you specifically?
```

Key rules:
- Always permission-based. Ask if they have a moment.
- Never start pitching without permission.
- Keep tone conversational and level. No enthusiasm overload.
- If they say no: "Totally understand, is there a better time to call back?" and move on.

## Step 2: Question (Subject Matter Expertise)

Once you have permission, jump straight into expertise. Reference common challenges other leaders in their position face.

```
Typically, when I talk to other [Sales Leaders / VPs of Sales / Agency Owners] 
that focus on outbound, there's one thing that's top of mind:

[Challenge: e.g. "Their reps spend hours on LinkedIn prospecting but the conversations 
don't convert to meetings."]

Is that the same for you, or is something else more important at the moment?
```

Key rules:
- Show you know their world. Reference a specific, common challenge.
- End with an open question that invites them to share their reality.
- If they share a different challenge, pivot to that. Don't force your script.
- Listen more than you talk in this step.

## Step 3: Close (Go for the Meeting)

Once you hear the challenge or get interest, close. We want a meeting booked.

```
That's actually something I hear from a lot of sales teams I speak with and 
something we can help with.

For context, your team will be able to continue their day to day work but have 
us working in the background creating conversations 24/7. That way they can 
focus on their day to day activities while automating their prospecting.

Would you be open to taking a quick look and seeing how it all works?
```

Key rules:
- Validate their challenge first ("I hear that a lot").
- Keep the product description concrete but brief.
- CTA is always a meeting, not a sale.
- If they say yes, book immediately. Don't keep pitching.

## Step 4: Discovery (Post-Book)

Meeting is booked. Now try to push light discovery on the same call to prep for the demo.

**Format A: Before Hang-Up**
```
Hey {name}, before you hang up, do you have a minute for a few quick questions 
so we make the most of the call tomorrow?
```

**Format B: Quick Two Questions**
```
Hey {name}, mind if I ask 2 quick questions so we can get the most out of 
our call this week?
```

Discovery questions to ask:
- How many reps are on the team?
- What are they currently using for LinkedIn outreach?
- What does a good month of pipeline look like for them?
- What's their biggest bottleneck in prospecting right now?

Key rules:
- Keep it light. 2-3 questions max.
- If they're in a hurry, let them go. You have the meeting booked, that's the win.
- Log everything in HubSpot immediately after the call.

## Objection Handling

All objections follow the same framework: Validate > Pivot > Re-ask.

"I hear that all the time and totally understand..." then pivot.

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "I'm not interested" | "I hear that all the time and totally understand. Most sales leaders aren't looking to change things. If it's alright with you, can I get 30 seconds to share the #1 way we help, and then you can let me know if it makes sense for us to keep chatting?" |
| "Send me an email" | "I hear that all the time and totally understand. Most sales leaders are busy and email is pretty fast. Would you be open to a 15 minute demo to show why I called you specifically?" |
| "We're already using X" | "I hear that all the time and totally understand. That's a great solution that a lot of sales leaders love. If it's alright with you, can I get 30 seconds to share our biggest differentiator, and then you can let me know if you're interested at all?" |
| "We don't have budget" | "I hear that all the time and totally understand. Most sales leaders don't have budget right now. If it's alright with you, can I get 30 seconds to share how our platform would maximize your existing investments?" |
| "We're all set" | "I hear that all the time and totally understand. Most sales leaders already have something in place. If it's alright with you, can I get 30 seconds to share our biggest differentiator and you let me know if it's interesting at all?" |

Key rules:
- Always validate first. Never argue or push back directly.
- One pivot attempt only. If they still say no, thank them and end gracefully.
- "30 seconds" framing works because it's a small ask.
- Never be pushy or aggressive. Professional persistence, not pressure.

## Voicemails

When you hit voicemail, leave a short message that drives them to email.

**Structure: Trigger > Email Subject > Teaser > No Callback**

```
Hey {name}, just sent you an email. Subject line: JUST CALLED.

Had some insights on how we can maximize the team's funnel. 
Thought you'd find it helpful.

No need to call me back. Just respond to that email instead.

Look forward to connecting.
```

Key rules:
- Always send a companion email with subject "JUST CALLED" before or immediately after leaving the voicemail.
- Keep the voicemail under 30 seconds.
- Always redirect them to email, not a callback. Lower friction.
- Tease value but don't pitch. The email carries the pitch.
- Warm, professional tone. Not rushed, not salesy.
